# STRATEGY_PRACTICE — AMI Assist as a Long-Duration Serious Practice

*A deliberate alternative to STRATEGY_COMPOUND. This document does not argue that the compound-infrastructure plan is wrong. It argues that there is a different game with different scoring: treat AMI Assist as a long-duration serious practice — closer to how a physician builds a clinical reputation, a researcher builds a program, or a craftsperson builds a body of work — rather than as a venture-backed startup racing moats.*

*The two documents are not opposed. They are different answers to different life questions. STRATEGY_COMPOUND asks: "if I optimize for moat-protected ten-figure outcomes, what would I do?" This document asks: "if I optimize for a decade of meaningful, coherent, personally-sustainable work that contributes something real — with commercial outcomes as welcome byproducts rather than obligations — what would I do?"*

*Both are legitimate. Neither is objectively better. The choice between them is the founder's, and it is a life-shape question more than a strategy question.*

---

## 0. For the reader — what this document is and isn't

**What this document is.** A plan for running AMI Assist as a long-duration serious practice over 10-20 years, alongside the founder's clinical work, with slow organic commercialization, no venture capital, no scaling targets, no stage gates tied to outside money, and no growth imperative. The operating model is closer to a senior clinician's specialty practice + a researcher's program + a craftsperson's shop, combined — sustained by the founder's clinical income, modest direct revenue from a small number of clinics, and occasional grant funding for specific experiments.

**What this document is not.** A strawman of the startup path. A justification for not trying. An argument that commercialization is bad. A rejection of ambition. A document about working less — if anything, serious practice demands *more* discipline than startup mode, because there is no external pressure to compensate for drift.

**Who this is written for.** A founder who already has a good professional base (clinical practice in AMI's case), finds the startup lifecycle culturally mismatched, wants to spend the next decade doing work they respect, is willing to sacrifice ten-figure outcomes for integrity of process, and has the self-discipline to stay productive without institutional pressure.

**Who this is not written for.** A founder who wants speed, team-scaling, VC validation, or the specific kind of success that requires startup mode. For that reader, STRATEGY_COMPOUND is the better document. The two plans are cleanly incompatible — attempting them simultaneously produces the worst of both.

**The honest comparison.** If executed well, STRATEGY_COMPOUND can produce a $250-400M Canadian healthcare infrastructure company with 100+ employees, meaningful contribution to how health is delivered, and a venture-style exit in Year 8-12. If executed well, STRATEGY_PRACTICE produces a working platform serving 5-30 clinics over 10-20 years, 2-4 peer-reviewed papers, well-designed hardware deployed at small scale, strong standing in Canadian clinical AI community, open-source contributions, sustained craft development, and a founder life that remained coherent throughout. These are different outcomes for different people.

## 1. The frame — "long-duration serious practice"

The language matters. "Hobby" understates the seriousness; "startup" overstates the intensity; "side project" captures neither the commitment nor the craft. The right frame is **long-duration serious practice**.

### 1.1 The three analogues

**Clinical practice.** A family physician in year 23 of practice has a specific kind of professional existence. They see patients most days; they teach trainees sometimes; they contribute to the evidence base occasionally through small studies or case reports; they maintain their craft through CME and collegial relationships; they serve a community over decades; they build reputation slowly through consistent competent work. They do not "scale." They also do not drift — the work has intrinsic structure and accountability.

**Research program.** A senior researcher at a university runs a program for 20-30 years. They take on specific questions, publish at a sustainable pace, train students, secure grants intermittently, follow threads as curiosity warrants. They are not on a 3-year product cycle. They also have real standards — peer review, replication, the slow accumulation of actual knowledge.

**Craftsperson's shop.** A master instrument maker, small-batch furniture maker, or senior software engineer working on SQLite or ffmpeg for decades. Their work is held to a quality standard nobody else imposes; they produce slowly but well; they serve a specific customer base; they are not trying to disrupt their industry.

AMI Assist in this frame is a combination of all three. The founder is a clinician running a technical practice parallel to the clinical one, contributing through research collaborations, maintaining a specific piece of software + hardware with craft-level attention, for a small circle of users the founder knows personally.

### 1.2 Why this frame fits the founder's situation specifically

The frame is not universally correct. It fits this specific situation because:

- **The founder has a working clinical practice** that funds life regardless of what happens to AMI Assist. This is the single most important enabling fact. Without an independent income base, this frame collapses into "underfunded startup."
- **The codebase already exists** and is being used daily in the founder's own clinic. The platform has demonstrated usefulness, which is the first hurdle most hobby projects never clear.
- **Canadian physician economics** support 15-20 hours a week of sustained technical work without financial strain. US physician economics mostly do not (higher expected work hours, higher malpractice costs, different tax structure). Canadian family medicine specifically has been moving toward team-based care + FHO+ compensation that leaves cognitive headroom for projects like this.
- **The software commoditization trend helps the slow builder.** AI-assisted coding means one disciplined person can do what five used to. This is an advantage the startup plan struggles to monetize (because competitors have it too) but that the slow-practice plan benefits from asymmetrically: the solo practitioner gets more productive each year without having to scale a team.
- **The founder's temperament** matters. Some people thrive on startup intensity. Others are drained by it. Honest self-knowledge about this is the decisive input. The founder's pattern of careful architectural decisions, depth over breadth, and multi-document strategic thinking suggests a researcher-craftsman temperament rather than a fast-execution operator temperament.

### 1.3 What the frame is not

**Not "lifestyle business."** That term implies reduced ambition and lower standards. This frame has very high standards — they are just applied over a 20-year horizon rather than a 5-year one. A lifestyle business is a coffee shop; a long-duration practice is a vineyard that takes 15 years before the first great vintage.

**Not "hobby."** A hobby has no accountability and no external users. This frame has real users (the clinics), real accountability (they depend on the platform working), and real craft standards (the founder's own professional reputation in a small community).

**Not "failure mode of startup."** This is a deliberate choice made in advance, not a consolation when the startup path doesn't work. Treating it as a failure mode corrupts it — the founder ends up running a slow-motion startup, which has all the burdens of startup life with none of the upside.

**Not "not commercial."** The plan has commercial activity — per-clinic licensing, grants, paid collaborations. What it lacks is *growth obligation*. Commercial activity is welcome, not required.

## 2. Why this frame might actually be right for AMI Assist

Three positive arguments for this frame over STRATEGY_COMPOUND, beyond pure preference:

### 2.1 The work benefits from patience

Medical software that people actually use for serious clinical work benefits from years of iteration with real users. The scribing quality in AMI Assist today is better than it was six months ago because of accumulated feedback from three real clinical deployments. Doubling the team would not double the feedback rate; the rate-limiting input is clinical reality, not engineering hours.

Many of the most interesting open problems in the platform (encounter detection, SOAP quality, multi-patient scenarios, vision-based patient identification) become tractable only after years of seeing edge cases. A startup sprint through these problems produces a shallower solution than patient iteration over a decade. The slow plan *is* the right technical approach for several of the platform's core capabilities.

### 2.2 The Canadian healthcare context rewards tenure more than speed

FHO physicians choose platforms through trusted peer networks over years. Provincial health authorities evaluate vendors on multi-year track records. Academic collaborators commit to researchers they've known for a decade. None of this is accelerated by fundraising announcements or aggressive scaling. A founder who has been quietly serving 10 clinics well for 8 years has a different standing in this community than a well-funded newcomer who has been serving 100 clinics poorly for 2 years.

The startup plan's answer to this is "we'll hire the right Canadian people to build that standing on our behalf." That works partially. It does not fully replace the founder's own two-decade presence in the community. If the founder is willing to *be* that presence directly, the slow plan produces trust that capital cannot.

### 2.3 Most important software is built this way

The examples are worth naming. SQLite has been maintained by Richard Hipp and a tiny team for 25+ years, is in the public domain, and runs in virtually every device in the world. ffmpeg was developed by Fabrice Bellard largely as a solo effort over decades and underpins most video infrastructure. TeX by Knuth took a decade and defined academic typesetting. Linux started as a student project and remained under Linus Torvalds' coordination for 30+ years. Rails came from a small team at 37signals without VC and shaped a generation of web development. BBEdit has been shipped continuously since 1992 by a tiny team at Bare Bones Software.

None of these are lifestyle businesses. All of them took their craft seriously over decades. All of them have outsized impact. Their authors did not need to scale teams or raise capital to produce work that mattered.

Medical software specifically has fewer such examples because healthcare's commercial structure punished independent developers historically (distribution required institutional relationships). AI-assisted coding + lowering deployment barriers + the open-source interoperability push in Canada may change that. AMI Assist could plausibly be to Canadian primary care ambient AI what SQLite became to databases: maintained by a very small team with high craft standards, for decades, contributing something durable.

### 2.4 The existential risk profile is different

Startup mode has binary failure modes: funding runs out, a competitor wins, the team implodes, the market shifts, and the company ends. The moat-compound plan is designed to mitigate these, but the mitigations are themselves bets.

Long-duration practice has gradual failure modes: the founder loses interest, the technology becomes obsolete, a core collaborator moves on. But it does not have cliff-edge failure. A bad year reduces output; it does not end the project. This is a more survivable existence structurally.

### 2.5 The founder's life remains whole

Startup mode, honestly executed, consumes a decade of the founder's life. Family, health, friendships, intellectual breadth, and the clinical practice itself all absorb costs to make the startup succeed. The reward for success is wealth and impact; the reward for failure is having spent a decade on something that didn't work.

Long-duration practice preserves the rest of life by design. The founder sees patients, spends time with family, reads widely, develops non-AMI interests, stays physically healthy, ages with grace. The platform is one part of a full life, not the life. When the founder reaches 70, they have spent 25 years on AMI Assist *and also* 40 years as a physician, raised a family, read a thousand books, stayed in shape, and maintained friendships. That is a different legacy than "founded a healthcare infrastructure company in my 40s."

Neither legacy is better. But the founder gets to choose.

## 3. What you give up

A serious document must name the costs honestly. This frame gives up real things.

### 3.1 Ten-figure outcomes

The $1-5B exit scenarios in STRATEGY_COMPOUND are not available in this frame. The platform will not IPO. It will likely not be acquired for nine figures, because its scale will never justify that valuation. If the founder's measure of success includes wealth at the ten-figure scale, this frame fails by definition.

### 3.2 Category leadership

Being "the" Canadian ambient AI platform, cited in practice guidelines, named in every conversation about clinical AI in Canada, winning conference keynotes, setting policy agendas — none of these are available at small scale. Tali AI, Dragon Copilot, Abridge, Nabla, and whatever enters the space will occupy those positions. AMI Assist will be respected by a small circle and unknown outside it. That is a real cost if recognition matters to the founder.

### 3.3 Scale of direct impact

A platform serving 10-30 clinics helps hundreds to low-thousands of patients. A platform serving 500+ clinics helps millions. The Mary-2036 scenario from STRATEGY_DECADE does not happen at 10 clinics. If the founder's measure of success is "how many patients had a better outcome because this existed," the slow plan scores lower by 2-3 orders of magnitude.

Mitigation: open-source contribution can extend impact beyond direct users. A well-designed sensor firmware used by other projects may help more patients indirectly than AMI's own deployments. But this is supplementary, not a replacement.

### 3.4 The momentum that scale creates

Startups in motion attract talent, partnerships, credibility, and regulatory engagement in compounding ways. A 50-person company has conversations a 2-person company cannot. Investors introduce portfolio companies; press coverage creates serendipity; team hiring attracts more team. The slow plan has none of this. Every conversation is one conversation; every collaboration is deliberate; every deployment is hand-crafted.

The slow-plan analogue is *craftsman's network* — a smaller, denser set of relationships built over decades — but it is not the same as the broader serendipity of scale.

### 3.5 The thrill

Startup life has a specific kind of intensity that some people genuinely enjoy. The pace of shipping, the high-stakes decisions, the visible growth numbers, the social signaling of "building something real" — these are legitimate goods. The slow plan does not offer them. Its pleasures are quieter and more accretive: the satisfaction of a problem solved well, a paper published, a clinic that has renewed for eight years, a clean piece of code that will still be running in 2040.

If the founder would miss the startup thrill, this frame is a mismatch. If the founder would find it a relief to escape, this frame fits.

### 3.6 Financial runway independence (conditional)

The slow plan depends on the clinical practice funding life. If the founder stops seeing patients, or provincial compensation shifts unfavorably, or a health event forces reduced clinical work, the platform loses its financial base. The startup plan, if it reaches Series A, has independent runway. The slow plan is permanently dependent on the clinical income.

This is a specific lifestyle coupling: you cannot retire from clinical medicine without this project also winding down, unless the platform has found other economic support by that point. Some founders will find this grounding; others will find it constraining.

## 4. The five principles of the practice

Operating commitments that substitute for stage gates and revenue targets.

### 4.1 Usefulness before growth

Every engineering decision is evaluated by "does this make the platform more useful to the users who already have it, or to colleagues who would use it if it existed?" not "does this help us acquire more users." The former produces deep useful software; the latter produces bloat and distraction.

In practice: say no to features that 10% of potential users want but current users don't need. Say yes to features that your 10 users would find life-changing even if no new users ever arrive.

### 4.2 Sustainability before intensity

The work must be doable indefinitely at current founder effort level. Sprints are allowed occasionally (2-3 weeks a year of concentrated focus) but the default pace must be sustainable without burnout, without sacrificing the clinical practice, without disrupting family or health.

In practice: 10-20 hours a week of platform work, plus dogfooding during clinical work, plus asynchronous thinking during walks, showers, and commutes. Not 60 hours a week.

### 4.3 Craft before completeness

A feature done well, slowly, is worth more than three features done quickly and badly. The platform can remain narrower than competitors as long as what it does, it does with rare care. Software read years later by a serious reader should still impress.

In practice: if you do not have time to do something well, do not do it. "Good enough for shipping" is still a quality floor that the slow plan preserves.

### 4.4 Community before market

The people who matter are a small set of clinicians, a smaller set of technical peers, and a smaller set of academic collaborators. The opinions that count are theirs. The recognition that matters is theirs. Market-facing events (conferences, press, industry awards) are tools, not goals. Academic-facing events (CFPC research day, clinical AI symposiums, university seminars) are the real medium.

In practice: prefer a paper or a talk at a small academic venue over a blog post aimed at the general tech audience. Prefer a deep conversation with a family physician over a marketing email to 500 clinics.

### 4.5 Curiosity before strategy

Follow interesting problems. If the platform can either build yet another integration for the 11th Canadian EMR or investigate a specific clinical pattern that seems to produce interesting signal — pursue the signal. Strategic correctness is overrated in this frame. The best work comes from following questions that refuse to leave the founder's mind.

In practice: keep a notebook of questions. When choosing what to work on, prefer the question that has been nagging for weeks over the one that would drive revenue.

## 5. What gets built — the narrow product focus

The platform is one thing, done well, rather than many things done halfway. In this frame:

### 5.1 Core: the clinical scribe for Canadian primary care

This remains the center of gravity. The platform is a high-quality ambient scribe for Canadian FHO family physicians, integrated with Oscar Pro, deployed at a small number of clinics the founder knows personally. Feature development continues indefinitely based on clinical feedback. The scribe should be plausibly the best-executed Canadian primary care scribe available, even if not the most-deployed.

Specific ongoing work:
- Continue iterating on encounter detection, SOAP quality, multi-patient handling
- Improve French Canadian + bilingual support as clinics request it
- Deepen specialty support (pain, MSK, chronic disease) for specific clinical questions
- Keep the codebase clean and readable; refuse to let it calcify

### 5.2 Secondary: the sensor work as craft hardware

The room sensor productization continues, but on a craft-scale timeline. Over 3-5 years (not 18 months), the ESP32 sensor becomes a small-batch-manufactured Canadian hardware product. Target: 100-500 units deployed total over a decade, not 50,000+.

This is enough to:
- Genuinely test whether ambient sensing improves the scribing experience
- Produce hardware work of quality the founder can be proud of
- Contribute open-source firmware that other projects can use
- Establish a small Canadian medical-hardware footprint

This is not enough to:
- Be a hardware business
- Drive regulatory moats at scale
- Produce 510(k) clearances across multiple indications
- Support a wearable, compute appliance, or custom silicon program

### 5.3 Tertiary: one research collaboration at a time

At any given time, one active academic collaboration. Current collaboration matures into a paper every 2-4 years (not every 12-18 months). Topics are chosen by what the data actually supports, not by what would be most commercially strategic.

Possible collaborations over a decade:
- Billing accuracy study with a Canadian family medicine department (Y1-3)
- Clinician burden study with a primary care research network (Y3-6)
- Specific clinical signal study (e.g., depression screening from ambient audio) with a Canadian psychiatry or primary care group (Y5-8)
- Long-term platform-deployment outcomes study (Y7-10)

The papers themselves are the output. Citation counts, downstream guideline inclusion, and commercial impact are welcomed byproducts if they arrive, but are not targets.

### 5.4 What explicitly does not get built

- Home monitoring at scale (intrinsically requires field-operations business)
- Wearables (intrinsically requires hardware-company operations)
- Compute appliances (operational burden disproportionate to value at this scale)
- Clinical trials operating mode (intrinsically different business)
- Hospital deployment (intrinsically different institutional landscape)
- International expansion (intrinsically requires team + regulatory stack)
- Custom silicon (not remotely sensible at this scale)
- Multi-patient / family portal (creates third-party consent complexity disproportionate to value at this scale)
- Enterprise sales (antithetical to the operating model)

This is not forever — any of these could become interesting in year 15 if circumstances warrant. But they are not part of the default plan.

## 6. The rhythm — how the weeks and years actually look

Abstract principles mean little without concrete operating patterns. Specifics:

### 6.1 The week

A typical week:
- Monday through Thursday: clinical work (~32 hours of patient care)
- Friday: 6-8 hours of platform work (deep-focus day)
- Weekends: 4-8 hours of platform work depending on family rhythm; at least one day fully off
- Evenings: occasional 1-2 hour sessions for small tasks, documentation, correspondence
- Early mornings: 30-60 minutes of reading (medical journals, technical writing, non-work)

Total platform work: 12-18 hours a week sustained, flexing to 25 during sprint weeks, flexing to 5 during low weeks (travel, holidays, life events).

### 6.2 The month

A typical month has:
- One deep Friday-and-weekend pair where something substantial ships
- Two or three Fridays of ongoing iteration, bug fixes, support
- One Friday off (in lieu of a weekend work session, alternating)
- A monthly check-in with an active academic collaborator
- A monthly reflection pass: what worked, what didn't, what to do next

### 6.3 The year

A typical year has:
- Two or three 2-week "sprint" blocks where the founder takes vacation from clinical work and works on the platform full-time
- Four quarterly planning sessions (2-3 hours each) where direction is reconsidered
- One annual writing pass: updating strategy documents, writing a paper, writing a blog post or essay
- One conference or symposium attended (CFPC, CAMIA, clinical AI at a Canadian university)
- One collaboration milestone (paper draft, study enrollment complete, grant submission)
- Continuous small deployments at existing clinics; one new clinic added every 6-18 months by direct invitation

### 6.4 The decade

- Y1-3: the platform becomes genuinely excellent at the core scribing task. Room sensor v1 enters small-batch production. 8-15 clinics using it. First paper submitted.
- Y3-6: a specific interesting clinical signal is pursued (encounter-level patterns, specialty-specific features, a home-observation experiment with 2-3 consenting patients). One paper published, one in progress.
- Y6-9: a second sensor (probably the home room sensor or a wearable integration — not in-house) added. A deeper collaboration with a Canadian academic center. A small installation among colleagues' home practices. 15-25 clinics using it.
- Y9-12: the platform is now a recognized regional artifact. The founder considers whether to pass stewardship to a successor, open-source more heavily, or continue solo.

This is not a trajectory. It is a sketch. Real progress will be nonlinear, with some years producing more and others producing less. The commitment is to the long arc, not to annual outputs.

### 6.5 What a low year looks like

Some years will produce very little — a sabbatical year, a year with a family health event, a year where the founder's clinical life demands more attention. In this frame, that is fine. The platform persists. Users continue to use it. Support continues. New development pauses. The frame specifically allows for this because the startup plan specifically cannot.

The test of the plan: the platform at the end of a low year is still working, still being paid for, and the founder is still interested in returning when the slow year ends. If either of those fails, the plan needs adjustment — not speed-up, but honest reflection on whether the work is still the right shape.

## 7. The economic model — modest, durable, uncomplicated

Money in this frame serves the work. It is not a metric, not a target, not a signal. It is sufficient when it covers the work's costs and contributes modestly to the founder's life without requiring growth.

### 7.1 Revenue sources

**Per-clinic licensing.** 10-30 clinics paying $2,000-4,000 per clinic per year (not per physician per month). Single annual invoice, no complicated billing. Total annual revenue: $20,000-100,000 at steady state. Covers hosting, modest development costs, incidental professional expenses.

**Occasional consulting.** The founder occasionally consults for academic groups, small health tech companies, or government programs on specific questions (interoperability, clinical AI evaluation, primary care informatics). Several thousand dollars per engagement, a few per year. Supplements revenue, keeps the founder engaged with the wider ecosystem.

**Grants.** IRAP for sensor productization (one grant of $200-500K over 2-3 years). Possible CIHR or NSERC support for specific clinical studies (smaller, collaboration-dependent). University-matched funding for research work. These cover specific projects, not ongoing operations.

**No funding rounds. No equity sold. No acquisition events.** These are actively declined.

### 7.2 Cost structure

- Hosting (cloud infrastructure, domain, SSL, monitoring): $5-10K/year
- Hardware (sensors, dev boards, small-batch production): $5-20K/year in sensor-development years, lower otherwise
- Software tools (IDEs, AI coding assistants, design tools, research subscriptions): $2-4K/year
- Professional services (accounting, incorporation maintenance): $3-5K/year
- Conference travel, books, CME: $3-8K/year
- Legal (occasional consult on privacy, licensing, contracts): $2-5K/year

Total annual operating cost at steady state: $20-50K/year. Covered by revenue in normal years; covered by grants in hardware-development years.

### 7.3 The founder's economic stance

Platform income is not the founder's living — the clinical practice is. AMI Assist contributes modest additional income and, more importantly, covers its own costs. It does not subsidize the founder's lifestyle. It does not finance a team. It does not fund speculation.

The founder is effectively a patron of their own project: a senior professional whose stable income supports sustained technical work that would be financially irrational on its own terms but produces work worth making.

### 7.4 What "enough" looks like

At year 5:
- Platform covers its own hosting and infrastructure
- Founder has not lost money personally
- Clinic users are satisfied; one or two are actively engaged advocates
- One paper published, one in submission
- Sensor v1 in small-batch production with 50+ units deployed
- Founder's clinical practice, family life, and health are intact

At year 10:
- Revenue of $50-100K/year
- 15-25 clinic users, mostly renewed for 5+ years
- 2-3 papers published; a third in progress
- Sensor family expanded to 2-3 devices, 200-500 units deployed total
- Open-source firmware referenced by a few other projects
- The platform has a known standing in Canadian clinical AI community
- The founder has a body of work they respect

At year 20:
- Platform still in operation or gracefully transitioned to a successor / open-source maintainer
- 5-10 papers published across the two decades
- Clinical deployment that outlasted a generation of startup competitors
- A discrete but real contribution to how Canadian primary care adopted ambient AI
- The founder, now approaching retirement from clinical practice, can choose whether to continue

These are modest outcomes compared to STRATEGY_COMPOUND's ambitious ones. They are also markedly more probable.

## 8. Collaborators — few, chosen carefully, kept for years

The plan does not have employees. It has collaborators — specific people whose involvement makes the work better and who are themselves well-served by the relationship.

### 8.1 The three types that fit

**Clinical collaborators.** Two to five physicians who use the platform seriously and give detailed feedback. Ideally one or two outside the founder's immediate region (to test whether the platform works for practices unlike the founder's). Compensation: use of the platform at steep discount or no charge, co-authorship on relevant papers, genuine engagement with their feature requests. Not employees.

**Technical peers.** One or two other senior software developers or engineers, each working on their own project, with regular mutual-review exchanges. The relationship is peer, not hierarchical. They don't work for AMI Assist; they cross-pollinate with it. This is how much of the best slow-built software has historically been developed — Knuth had his correspondents, Hipp has his reviewers, Bellard had the wider FFmpeg community.

**Academic partners.** One active research collaboration at a time, with a specific academic in a specific institution on a specific question. Time-bound by the specific paper or study. When that project ends, the partnership may continue into a next project or gracefully end. The founder is a co-investigator or data contributor, not a contractor.

### 8.2 The three types that don't fit

**Employees.** Having employees means salaries, benefits, HR, performance management, firing conversations, succession planning. The slow plan does not have the management capacity or the desire to support these. If the founder wants to hire, the answer is usually "don't" — hire a contractor for a specific discrete project instead, or find a collaborator who fits one of the three types above.

**Co-founders.** A co-founder expects equity, voice, governance, and significant time commitment. The slow plan is singular — the founder's project — and adding a co-founder restructures it into something closer to a startup. If a genuinely great co-founder appears, the right response is often to help them do their own project and collaborate loosely.

**Investors.** By definition external to this frame. Turning down investment offers politely and promptly is one of the operational disciplines this plan requires.

### 8.3 How the collaborative relationships are sustained

- Explicit agreements about contribution, compensation (if any), attribution, IP
- Regular but light-touch communication (monthly or quarterly check-ins, not weekly standups)
- Patience with each collaborator's own life and other work
- Genuine reciprocity — the collaborators should be getting something real, not just helping the founder
- Willingness to let collaborations end when they've run their course

### 8.4 The founder's own role as collaborator to other projects

A slow-plan founder is also available to contribute modestly to others' projects — reviewing papers, commenting on early-stage code, participating in standards discussions, occasional mentorship. This is part of being in the community, not a distraction from the core project. A practice exists within a field of other practices.

## 9. What gets refused — the discipline of saying no

The biggest operational risk in the slow plan is drift toward startup mode. Opportunities will appear. The plan's integrity depends on declining most of them.

### 9.1 Things to decline

**Venture capital.** Any conversation that starts with "we'd like to invest" or "we see the opportunity here" should end with a polite thanks and no. Even a single VC investor on the cap table changes the game. Once capital expects return, the slow plan collapses.

**Acquisition offers below founder's decision threshold.** The default is "not interested." Only a specific kind of acquisition makes sense in this frame — one where the acquirer's stewardship would genuinely continue the work and the founder wants to stop. Most acquisition offers will not fit. The default answer is no; the exception requires careful thought.

**Enterprise sales cycles.** The platform does not sell to hospitals, health systems, or provincial procurements. Those engagements are 12-24 month processes that require sales engineering, legal, security review, implementation services, and ongoing account management. None of these exist in the slow plan. If a large institution asks to buy, the right answer is "I'm not set up to support you well; here's another vendor that is."

**Regulatory programs requiring full-time compliance staff.** 510(k) submissions beyond a very narrow Class I device, FDA inspections, ISO 13485 QMS at scale — all require compliance infrastructure the slow plan cannot support. Narrow, specific regulatory work that matches one sensor with one indication is fine. Broad regulatory ambition is not.

**Advisory boards, board seats, time-consuming speaking circuits.** Serving on someone else's board, giving monthly keynotes, being on industry committees. All worthwhile in small doses; all distractions at larger volumes. Limit: 1-2 per year of anything that demands recurring time commitment.

**Features driven by user acquisition.** "If we built X, we could reach Y more clinics" is the wrong question in this frame. The right question is "does X help the users we already have?"

**Press cycles, social media content strategies, marketing-driven content.** A website that clearly describes what the platform does is sufficient. Content marketing as a strategy is a startup practice. The slow plan's marketing is: do good work, publish when meaningful, speak when invited to specific audiences, let word of mouth propagate at its natural rate.

### 9.2 Things to accept

**Academic collaboration invitations** (if matched to current interests and capacity)
**Invitations to write or speak at specific medical/technical venues** (in moderate volume)
**Peer review requests** for papers in the founder's area (good citizenship)
**Introductions to interested clinicians** (slow conversion to users is the right growth rate)
**Grant opportunities** for specific work that fits existing direction
**Open-source contributions** from others, if the quality matches platform standards

### 9.3 Things to actively seek

**Clinical feedback from existing users** (the primary input signal)
**Reading time** for serious books, papers, essays (not hot takes and social media)
**Time to think** — the slow plan's most underrated operational practice
**Relationships with 5-10 people in the Canadian clinical AI space** cultivated over a decade

## 10. Slow commercialization — revenue as proof, not purpose

Commercial activity exists in the slow plan. It is just instrumentally different.

### 10.1 The pricing model

Annual per-clinic license. $2,500-3,500/year per clinic as a typical rate. Volume discounts for multi-clinic groups (but multi-clinic groups are rare at this scale, so this mostly doesn't come up). Invoice once a year; no usage tracking beyond aggregate anonymized stats.

Why flat pricing: minimizes billing overhead; sets the platform as a durable utility; aligns incentives so the founder is not motivated to add features that increase usage-based charges; is recognizable to clinic administrators as similar to other professional subscriptions.

Why this specific range: covers hosting + modest development contribution; is below the psychological threshold that triggers formal procurement processes; is fair to the founder without requiring salary-level revenue per clinic.

### 10.2 The sales process

There is no sales process. New clinic users arrive through:
- Word of mouth from existing users
- Direct invitation from the founder to colleagues
- Occasional referrals from academic collaborators
- The rare case where a physician has seen the platform at a conference or in writing and reaches out

Conversion: usually a conversation, a demo during a real clinic session, a free trial of 1-3 months, and then either a yes-or-no. No pipelines, no CRM, no follow-up sequences.

### 10.3 The support model

Email-based support from the founder directly. Same-business-day response during weekdays; slower on weekends. For serious issues, a phone call. For routine questions, asynchronous.

This works at the 10-30 clinic scale. At 100+ clinics, the founder would need support staff, which requires becoming an employer, which restructures the whole plan. The natural ceiling of the slow plan is therefore around 30 clinics — a useful constraint, not a limitation.

### 10.4 Contracting

Simple one-page annual agreement. Payment terms, confidentiality, data handling, termination with 30 days' notice, limitation of liability, governing law (Ontario). No complex SaaS MSA, no customization, no addenda. A physician clinic administrator can read and sign in 10 minutes.

For clinics that require more formal contracting — "our health authority requires a 40-page MSA with audit rights and insurance certificates" — the answer is often "we are not a good fit; here's a vendor that is."

### 10.5 Why this is still commercially real

$50-100K/year of recurring revenue from 15-25 clinics that have renewed for 5+ years is not trivial. It covers costs; it funds the sensor work; it signals durable usefulness; it is more resilient than most early-stage startup revenue because the customers chose carefully and the product is durable.

The slow plan's commercial minimum viable revenue — where the platform at least pays for itself and the founder does not lose money — is around $20K/year, achievable with 6-8 clinics. Below that, the clinical practice is subsidizing the platform; above that, the platform is a net positive. Neither failure.

## 11. Grants and research funding — the one large-scale external relationship

Grants fit the slow plan because they fund specific projects with clear deliverables and discrete timelines. They do not require team scaling or governance restructuring. They do add compliance overhead, which is worth planning for.

### 11.1 Grants that fit

**IRAP.** National Research Council's Industrial Research Assistance Program. Funds technology productization with clear commercialization path. A $300-500K IRAP grant over 18-24 months for room sensor productization is realistic and specifically structured for companies at this scale. The founder can administer this solo with modest accounting support.

**CIHR.** Canadian Institutes of Health Research. Funds clinical research. Most CIHR grants require academic partnership; the founder's role is co-investigator. The university administers the grant; AMI Assist contributes in-kind (platform access, data management, technical support) and sometimes receives a small subaward for specific work.

**NSERC CRD.** Collaborative Research and Development grants, matched by industry partner. Similar structure to CIHR — academic-led, industry as partner. Fits the slow plan when there is a specific research question worth pursuing.

**Provincial programs.** Ontario Centre of Innovation, Ontario SmartStart, BC Tech Fund, Quebec's Investissement Québec. Smaller, more specific, usually matched to particular milestones. Occasional fit.

**University-industry partnerships.** Sometimes a department has discretionary funds to support a technical collaborator's work. Not a formal grant; an informal arrangement. Can be very valuable if the relationship is right.

### 11.2 Grants that do not fit

**Large multi-year infrastructure grants** (NIH R01-scale in US, equivalent in Canada). These require grant administration at a level incompatible with solo operation.

**Venture-adjacent grants** (SBIR in US, specific sectoral programs with growth mandates). These come with growth expectations that conflict with the slow plan.

**Strategic initiatives** with specific commercialization milestones tied to headcount or revenue. Any grant whose success criteria require scaling is a trap.

### 11.3 The grant administrative discipline

Even well-scoped grants create compliance work — quarterly reports, progress summaries, expenditure tracking, in-kind documentation. The slow plan can absorb one active grant at a time. Beyond one, grant administration begins to compete with actual platform work. Discipline: do not start a second grant until the first is complete or near-complete.

## 12. Open source as dissemination

Open source serves the slow plan in ways it does not serve startups.

### 12.1 What to open-source

**Components where openness genuinely adds value:**
- Sensor firmware (ESP32 medical-grade sensor implementation, including board designs, firmware, provisioning tools)
- Specific clinical-AI tooling that others could benefit from (replay bundle schemas, audit architecture patterns)
- Integration shims for Canadian EMRs that should really be shared infrastructure
- Clinical evaluation frameworks (prompt evaluation, note quality metrics) that advance the field

**Components where openness does not help:**
- The specific trained models (not meaningfully useful without the rest of the stack)
- Clinical content (prompts, SOAP templates, billing rules) — these are the actual craft and are competitive
- Workflow-specific UX code (too idiosyncratic to be useful in isolation)

### 12.2 How to open-source

MIT or Apache 2.0 for maximum reusability. Clear README with honest description of what the code does, does not do, and is not meant for. Maintained with the same standards as the private codebase — no abandoned dumps. Small, curated contributions accepted; large architectural changes rarely.

### 12.3 Why this matters

Open-source components are how slow-built software contributes to the wider ecosystem beyond its direct users. A family physician in rural New Brunswick who cannot afford any ambient AI platform can still benefit if the sensor firmware they use (in a different project) was stabilized by AMI's work. A research group doing clinical AI evaluation can use AMI's replay schema as a starting point. The impact multiplies beyond what the founder's direct deployment reaches.

### 12.4 The time cost

Open source takes maintenance. Badly-maintained open source is worse than private code. Budget: 1-2 hours a week on open-source components, plus 8-16 hours a year of concentrated cleanup + release work. Do not open-source more than you can maintain at this standard.

## 14. Hardware in the slow frame — craft, not manufacturing

The sensor work continues, at craft scale.

### 14.1 What craft-scale hardware looks like

- Small-batch manufacturing at a Canadian CM (one of the tier-2 CMs, not Jabil)

- The sensor genuinely improves the platform's clinical capability
- The hardware work keeps the founder's skills sharp across software + firmware + physical design

- 50-500 units cannot accumulate a sensor-based moat
- Small-batch cost structure cannot compete on price at scale
- Installation-by-founder model does not scale
- Class I registration does not support the regulatory ambitions of STRATEGY_COMPOUND

These are features, not bugs, of the slow plan. The ambition is matched to the capacity.

## 16. Risks specific to this frame

The slow plan has its own failure modes. Honest enumeration:

### 16.1 Drift — losing focus without external pressure

The single largest risk. Without funding pressure, customer pressure, or team accountability, the founder may gradually stop shipping, stop iterating, stop finishing things. Platform becomes stale; users lose trust; work degrades.

**Mitigation:** quarterly reviews with an honest external party (a technical peer, an academic collaborator, a spouse). Annual self-evaluation with specific "did I finish the things I said I would?" questions. Willingness to say "this year was bad" and recommit.

### 16.2 Obsolescence — the world moving past you

The ambient AI space will change dramatically over the next decade. A platform that stood still for 3 years could be irrelevant. The slow plan's defense: continuous dogfooding (the founder uses it daily) and small-scale iteration. But there is a real risk that what's good enough for 10 clinics in 2026 is inadequate for those same clinics' expectations in 2032.

**Mitigation:** set a minimum standard — the platform must keep pace with the capability improvements that matter to users. This is still much less than "keep pace with the competitive market" but more than "let it freeze."

### 16.3 Isolation — no team to push you

Startup teams have a kind of cognitive surround that solo practitioners don't. A team argues, catches each other's blindspots, keeps each other accountable. The slow plan's collaborators are lighter-weight; they cannot substitute for team density. The founder can drift intellectually without noticing.

**Mitigation:** active cultivation of the collaborator network. Reading widely in adjacent fields. Participating in small discussion groups, academic seminars, peer code reviews. Writing things that others might critique.

### 16.4 Financial drift — clinical income changes

The plan depends on the clinical practice funding life. Provincial compensation changes, health events, or the founder's own decision to reduce clinical work could erode the financial base. The platform then either becomes a cash drain or must pivot to a different economic model mid-decade.

**Mitigation:** the platform's revenue should cover its own costs (so if the clinical practice shrinks, the platform does not become a drain on savings). Maintain some financial slack — not in a startup "runway" sense but in a clinician-finance "buffer" sense. Be honest about the coupling.

### 16.5 The founder's interest genuinely changes

Ten years is a long time. The founder may, honestly, stop caring about AMI Assist at year 6. At that point, the plan's commitment loses its motive force. The clinicians depending on the platform are stuck.

**Mitigation:** the plan should have honest exit / transition provisions from the start. If interest wanes, the platform should either find a successor, open-source fully, or be gracefully retired with at least 12 months' notice to users. Unmanaged abandonment is the worst outcome.

### 16.6 The startup temptation

At some point, an opportunity will appear that makes the startup path suddenly attractive. A great team volunteers to join. A specific investor offers exactly the right terms. A market opportunity opens. The risk: the founder abandons the slow plan mid-stream, attempts the startup plan belatedly, and does neither well.

**Mitigation:** explicit pre-commitment to the frame. If the founder genuinely wants to pivot to startup mode, that decision requires 6 months of reflection, not 2 weeks. The default answer to "should we start raising?" is "no, not yet — let's think about it for a season."

### 16.7 The moat argument might actually be right

STRATEGY_COMPOUND's thesis — that software is commoditizing and non-software moats are what survives — is not wrong. If that thesis is correct, a slow-plan platform is structurally exposed to well-funded fast-moving competitors that build moats while the slow platform does not. Over a 10-year horizon, the slow plan may be out-competed in ways that a faster plan would have avoided.

**Mitigation:** the slow plan's answer is that it does not need to win the market. It needs to serve its 10-30 clinics well, maintain integrity, and contribute to the field. That remains achievable even in a market where a moat-built competitor wins most share. The plan's success criterion is not market share.

### 16.8 The craftsmanship argument might be wrong

A counter-critique of the slow plan: perhaps slow doesn't actually produce better work. Perhaps the competitive pressure of startup mode, the team density, the regular deadlines, produce software that the patient craftsman can't match. Certainly some domains — consumer product design, for instance — reward rapid iteration.

**Mitigation:** honest comparison. If over 5 years, competitor platforms clearly produce better clinical outcomes and the slow plan does not, the thesis is falsified and the plan needs revision. Keep humble about whether the craft argument is actually producing better work or just feels more comfortable.

### 17.1 The choice is life-shape, not strategy

The two plans aren't competing strategies — they're answers to different questions about what kind of decade the founder wants to live. Some specific tradeoffs:

- **If the founder wants ten-figure financial outcomes**, STRATEGY_COMPOUND is the only plausible path.
- **If the founder wants to remain a practicing physician throughout**, STRATEGY_PRACTICE is the natural path.
- **If the founder wants to maximize contribution to how healthcare is delivered in Canada**, STRATEGY_COMPOUND has higher potential but also higher failure risk; STRATEGY_PRACTICE has lower potential but higher probability.
- **If the founder wants to preserve family and health over the decade**, STRATEGY_PRACTICE is structurally friendlier.
- **If the founder is energized by building teams**, STRATEGY_COMPOUND provides that; STRATEGY_PRACTICE specifically avoids it.
- **If the founder values craft quality over scale**, STRATEGY_PRACTICE allows sustained attention to craft that STRATEGY_COMPOUND cannot.

### 17.2 The mixed strategy trap

Attempting both plans simultaneously is the worst outcome. A founder who tries to run STRATEGY_PRACTICE but takes a $5M seed ends up with investor pressure and no plan to deploy capital. A founder who attempts STRATEGY_COMPOUND while maintaining a full clinical practice cannot deliver the intensity required and falls behind competitors. Clarity about which plan is being executed is operationally critical.

**Decision discipline:** make the choice explicitly, in writing, with a 12-month minimum commitment before reconsidering. Revisit only at specific intervals (annually, or upon specific trigger events like an acquisition offer or a major life change).

## 21. Closing — why this might be the right decade

There is a version of the next 10 years where the founder builds the Canadian healthcare infrastructure company STRATEGY_COMPOUND describes. That life has clear rewards and clear costs. It produces a specific kind of person.

There is another version where the founder continues practicing medicine, keeps AMI Assist alive as a serious craft practice, writes slowly, teaches occasionally, publishes deliberately, maintains a platform that is genuinely excellent for the people who use it, contributes small durable things to open source, stays physically healthy, raises a family, reads widely, thinks clearly. The platform does not become famous. The founder becomes respected in a small circle. At 60, the founder is still practicing medicine and still shipping small improvements to a platform used by 20 clinics who have been with it for a decade. At 70, the founder is retiring from clinical practice, possibly transitioning AMI Assist to a younger steward, possibly continuing to tinker, possibly letting it rest.

Neither life is objectively better. The latter is, however, rarely recommended by strategy documents, pitched by advisors, or celebrated by tech culture. Strategy writing is disproportionately about the first life because that is the life that generates news.

A document that makes the second life possible to choose with full knowledge of its costs and benefits is a public good. This is that document.

The founder's job is to read both documents honestly and then live with the consequences of whichever choice is made.
